# DSA-3050_F
Problem Statement

Many students in online learning environments fail or withdraw from courses. This project aims to analyze how student demographics, academic background, and online engagement influence final course outcomes (Pass, Fail, Withdrawn, Distinction) using the OULAD dataset.

Objectives
	1.	To examine the relationship between demographic factors (gender, age, education level, region, disability) and final results.
	2.	To analyze how academic factors (previous attempts, credits studied, assessment scores) affect performance.
	3.	To evaluate whether VLE engagement (clicks and activity) influences student success.
	4.	To identify patterns of at-risk students who are likely to fail or withdraw.

Literature Review 

1. The Dataset
The Open University Learning Analytics Dataset (OULAD) is a popular collection of student information used for research. It's special because it combines two things: basic student details (like age and education) and a detailed record of their activity in the online learning environment (VLE). Because it has both types of data, studies using it have been very good at predicting student success.

2. How Background Affects Success
Research shows that a student's past education is a major clue to how they will do.
Past Education: Students with higher qualifications before starting a course are often better at managing their own study time.
Age and Background: Some researchers think basic details like age aren't very useful on their own. However, other studies show that combining these details with early test scores is a powerful way to spot students who might be at risk, and to do it early in the course.
Course Load: The number of credits a student takes can show their workload. Taking too many credits at once is often linked to a higher chance of dropping out.

3. What Students Do Online: A Key Warning Sign
Recent research focuses less on who the student is and more on what they actually do in the online learning system.
Digital Footprints: A student's clicks in the VLE like visiting forums, reading materials, or accessing different pages are better at predicting success than their age or background.
Study Habits: Students who get top grades tend to log in and study a little bit every day. Students who are struggling often study irregularly or have long breaks between logins. This supports the idea that online activity is a main factor in success.

4. Using Data to Take Action
Turning all this research into useful tools helps teachers help students.
Being Proactive: BI tools like Power BI help schools move from just reacting to problems to spotting them early. By putting student data into live dashboards, schools can set up automatic alerts when a student might be falling behind.
Seeing the Details: Using specific data models and measures, teachers can "drill down" to see exactly which group of students is having trouble in which specific class. This kind of detailed view is something regular spreadsheets just can't offer.
